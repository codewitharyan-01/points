Ethical dilemma: define/explain with example
5 Marks

Definition: An ethical dilemma is a situation where an individual faces a conflict between moral imperatives, in which to obey one would result in transgressing another. It's a choice between two or more options, neither of which resolves the situation in an ethically acceptable fashion.

Characteristics:

  • Involves a conflict between at least two moral values
  • There is no perfect solution
  • Requires careful consideration of all consequences
  • Often involves professional responsibilities conflicting with personal values

Example: A doctor discovers that a patient has a terminal illness but the family requests that the doctor not inform the patient to prevent emotional distress. The doctor faces an ethical dilemma between respecting the family's wishes and the patient's right to know about their own health condition.

Kohlberg's Model of Cognitive Moral Development
5 Marks

Overview: Lawrence Kohlberg's theory of moral development consists of three levels with two stages each, totaling six stages of moral reasoning.

Level 1: Pre-conventional Morality

  • Stage 1: Obedience and Punishment Orientation - Individuals focus on avoiding punishment. They see rules as fixed and absolute.
  • Stage 2: Individualism and Exchange - Individuals recognize that there is not just one right view. Different individuals have different viewpoints.

Level 2: Conventional Morality

  • Stage 3: Good Interpersonal Relationships - The individual is good in order to be seen as a good person by others.
  • Stage 4: Maintaining the Social Order - The individual becomes aware of the wider rules of society.

Level 3: Post-conventional Morality

  • Stage 5: Social Contract and Individual Rights - The individual becomes aware that while rules/laws might exist for the good of the greatest number, there are times when they will work against the interest of particular individuals.
  • Stage 6: Universal Principles - The individual has developed their own set of moral guidelines which may or may not fit the law.

Ethics of Swami Vivekananda
5 Marks

Overview: Swami Vivekananda's ethics were deeply rooted in Vedanta philosophy and emphasized the divinity of human beings and service to humanity as service to God.

Key principles of Vivekananda's ethics:

  • Divinity of the soul: He believed that every individual is divine and should be treated with respect and dignity.
  • Selfless service (Seva): He emphasized serving others without expecting anything in return as the highest form of worship.
  • Truth and honesty: He advocated for absolute truthfulness in thoughts, words, and actions.
  • Fearlessness: He encouraged people to be fearless and stand up for righteousness.
  • Universal brotherhood: He promoted the idea of universal brotherhood beyond caste, creed, and nationality.
  • Character building: He emphasized the importance of building a strong character based on moral values.
  • Self-realization: He believed that the ultimate goal of human life is self-realization, which requires ethical living.

Relevance today: Vivekananda's ethical teachings continue to inspire people to lead a life of purpose, service, and moral integrity, making them relevant in contemporary business and personal contexts.

Characteristics of moral standards
5 Marks

Moral standards have the following key characteristics:

  • Universal applicability: Moral standards are intended to apply to all persons in similar situations, regardless of personal preferences or cultural differences.
  • Based on rational considerations: They are not based on mere feelings, desires, or preferences but on sound reasons and logical arguments.
  • Overriding importance: Moral standards take precedence over other considerations, including self-interest, when determining what to do.
  • Not established by authority figures: The validity of moral standards does not depend on whether they are endorsed by particular authorities or institutions.
  • Associated with special emotions: When moral standards are violated, they typically evoke emotions like guilt, shame, and remorse.
  • Serve the public interest: Moral standards are concerned with promoting the well-being of all affected parties, not just the individual.
  • Impartiality: They require treating similar cases similarly and making decisions without favoritism or bias.
Law of Karma
5 Marks

Definition: The Law of Karma is a fundamental concept in Indian philosophy that states that every action has consequences. It is often described as the law of cause and effect, where actions (causes) create future consequences (effects).

Key aspects of the Law of Karma:

  • Cause and effect: Every action, thought, or word produces an effect that will return to the doer.
  • Moral accountability: Individuals are responsible for their actions and must face their consequences.
  • Cycle of rebirth: Karma operates across lifetimes, influencing one's future incarnations.
  • Types of Karma:
    • Sanchita Karma: Accumulated karma from past actions
    • Prarabdha Karma: Portion of sanchita karma that is bearing fruit in the present life
    • Agami Karma: Karma being created in the present life that will bear fruit in the future
  • Not deterministic: While karma creates tendencies, it doesn't completely determine one's future; free will and conscious choices can modify karmic effects.
  • Ethical implications: The law encourages ethical behavior by highlighting that good actions lead to positive consequences and harmful actions lead to suffering.

Relevance in ethics: The Law of Karma provides a framework for understanding moral responsibility and encourages ethical behavior by emphasizing that actions have long-term consequences for the individual and society.

White-collar crime
5 Marks

Definition: White-collar crime refers to non-violent crimes committed by business professionals, government officials, or individuals in positions of authority and trust, typically for financial gain.

Characteristics of white-collar crime:

  • Committed by individuals in high social status or positions of trust
  • Typically non-violent in nature
  • Often involves deception, concealment, or breach of trust
  • Usually financially motivated
  • Can cause significant economic harm to individuals, organizations, or society

Examples of white-collar crime:

  • Embezzlement - Misappropriation of assets entrusted to one's care
  • Fraud - Deception for personal gain
  • Insider trading - Using confidential information for stock market advantage
  • Money laundering - Concealing the origins of illegally obtained money
  • Bribery and corruption - Offering, giving, receiving, or soliciting something of value to influence actions
  • Tax evasion - Illegal avoidance of paying taxes
  • Cybercrime - Criminal activities carried out using computers or the internet

Ethical implications: White-collar crimes violate trust, undermine economic systems, and harm society. They often involve abuse of power and position, making them particularly concerning from an ethical perspective.

Principles of professional ethics
5 Marks

Key principles of professional ethics:

  • Integrity: Being honest and truthful in all professional relationships and activities. Professionals should not be associated with misleading information.
  • Objectivity: Professionals should not allow bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence of others to override professional judgments.
  • Professional competence and due care: Professionals have a duty to maintain professional knowledge and skills at the required level and act diligently in accordance with applicable technical and professional standards.
  • Confidentiality: Professionals should respect the confidentiality of information acquired during professional work and should not disclose such information to third parties without proper authority.
  • Professional behavior: Professionals should comply with relevant laws and regulations and avoid any action that discredits the profession.
  • Transparency: Being open and honest about professional processes, decisions, and actions.
  • Accountability: Taking responsibility for professional decisions and actions.
  • Respect for rules and regulations: Adhering to the laws, regulations, and standards that govern the profession.

Importance: These principles provide a framework for ethical decision-making in professional contexts and help maintain public trust in various professions.

Define ethics
5 Marks

Definition: Ethics is the branch of philosophy that involves systematizing, defending, and recommending concepts of right and wrong conduct. It addresses questions about morality, such as what is morally good and bad, right and wrong.

Key aspects of ethics:

  • Normative dimension: Ethics provides standards or norms for behavior that distinguish between right and wrong.
  • Systematic study: It involves a structured examination of moral principles and values.
  • Practical application: Ethics is concerned with real-world moral dilemmas and decision-making.
  • Rational justification: Ethical principles require logical reasoning and justification.

Branches of ethics:

  • Meta-ethics: Examines the nature of ethical properties and judgments.
  • Normative ethics: Develops ethical theories and standards that determine right and wrong.
  • Applied ethics: Addresses specific moral issues in practical contexts.

Importance: Ethics provides frameworks for resolving moral conflicts, guides personal and professional conduct, and contributes to the functioning of a just society.

Differences: personal ethics vs professional ethics
5 Marks

Personal Ethics:

  • Refers to the moral principles and values that guide an individual's behavior in personal life
  • Developed through family upbringing, education, personal experiences, and reflection
  • More subjective and may vary significantly between individuals
  • Not formally codified but may be influenced by religious, cultural, or philosophical beliefs
  • Applies to personal relationships, individual decisions, and private conduct

Professional Ethics:

  • Refers to the ethical standards and codes of conduct that govern behavior within a professional context
  • Often formally codified in professional codes of ethics, regulations, or organizational policies
  • More objective and standardized within a specific profession
  • Developed by professional organizations, regulatory bodies, or employers
  • Applies to professional relationships, workplace decisions, and business practices

Relationship between personal and professional ethics:

  • Personal ethics form the foundation for professional ethics
  • Conflicts may arise when personal ethics clash with professional requirements
  • Professional ethics often extend or specify personal ethics in work contexts
  • Both require integrity, honesty, and responsibility
Moral philosophy
5 Marks

Definition: Moral philosophy, also known as ethics, is the branch of philosophy that explores the nature of moral judgments, moral principles, and moral problems. It seeks to understand the foundations of ethical behavior and the criteria for right and wrong actions.

Major approaches in moral philosophy:

  • Virtue Ethics: Focuses on the character and virtues of the moral agent rather than on rules or consequences. Aristotle is a key proponent, emphasizing virtues like courage, temperance, and justice.
  • Deontological Ethics: Emphasizes duty and rules in determining moral actions. Immanuel Kant's categorical imperative is a classic example, stating that actions should be based on principles that could be universally applied.
  • Consequentialism/Utilitarianism: Judges actions based on their outcomes or consequences. Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill advocated for the greatest happiness principle, which suggests that the right action maximizes overall happiness or utility.
  • Rights-based Ethics: Centers on the rights of individuals and the moral obligations to respect those rights.
  • Contractarian Ethics: Views moral principles as arising from a social contract or agreement among rational individuals.

Contemporary relevance: Moral philosophy provides frameworks for addressing complex ethical issues in various fields, including business, medicine, technology, and public policy, helping individuals and societies navigate moral challenges.

Cross-holder conflict / holder conflict
5 Marks

Definition: Cross-holder conflict, also known as stakeholder conflict, occurs when the interests of different stakeholders in an organization are incompatible or in direct opposition. Stakeholders include any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the organization's objectives.

Types of stakeholders involved in conflicts:

  • Shareholders vs. Employees: Shareholders may seek higher profits through cost-cutting, while employees want better wages and job security.
  • Management vs. Shareholders: Managers might prioritize personal benefits or long-term growth, while shareholders may focus on short-term returns.
  • Company vs. Community: Business operations might benefit the company but harm the local environment or community interests.
  • Customers vs. Shareholders: Customers desire quality products at low prices, while shareholders seek maximum profit.
  • Suppliers vs. Company: Suppliers want fair prices and prompt payments, while the company aims to minimize costs.

Ethical approaches to resolving stakeholder conflicts:

  • Stakeholder theory: Recognizes the legitimate interests of all stakeholders and seeks balanced solutions.
  • Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR): Encourages businesses to consider social and environmental impacts alongside economic goals.
  • Ethical decision-making frameworks: Provide structured approaches to weigh competing interests and find fair resolutions.
  • Dialogue and negotiation: Open communication between stakeholders to understand concerns and find mutually acceptable solutions.

Indian ethical traditions
5 Marks

Overview: Indian ethical traditions are deeply rooted in ancient philosophical and religious systems that offer comprehensive frameworks for moral living and spiritual development.

Major Indian ethical traditions:

  • Vedic Ethics: Based on the concept of Dharma (righteous duty), emphasizing truth (Satya), non-violence (Ahimsa), and self-discipline.
  • Buddhist Ethics: Centered on the Eightfold Path, emphasizing right understanding, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration.
  • Jain Ethics: Focuses on non-violence (Ahimsa) in its most absolute form, truthfulness, non-stealing, chastity, and non-possession.
  • Sikh Ethics: Emphasizes honest living, sharing with others, meditation on God's name, and service to humanity.
  • Islamic Ethics in India: Based on principles of justice, compassion, honesty, and fulfillment of obligations.

Common themes in Indian ethical traditions:

  • Emphasis on duty (Dharma) over rights
  • Importance of self-control and discipline
  • Non-violence and respect for all life
  • Truthfulness and honesty
  • Detachment from material possessions
  • Service to others as a moral obligation
  • Harmony between individual and cosmic order

Contemporary relevance: These ethical traditions continue to influence Indian society and provide valuable insights for addressing modern ethical challenges in business, governance, and personal life.

Integrity, honesty, transparency in ethics
5 Marks

Integrity in Ethics:

  • Definition: Consistency between one's values, words, and actions
  • Involves adherence to moral and ethical principles in all circumstances
  • Requires moral courage to stand up for one's beliefs
  • Creates trust and credibility in personal and professional relationships
  • Example: A manager refusing to engage in corrupt practices despite pressure from superiors

Honesty in Ethics:

  • Definition: Truthfulness in words and actions
  • Involves not deceiving, misleading, or withholding relevant information
  • Requires sincerity and authenticity in communication
  • Fosters trust and open communication
  • Example: An employee admitting a mistake rather than covering it up

Transparency in Ethics:

  • Definition: Openness about processes, decisions, and actions
  • Involves making information accessible and understandable
  • Requires accountability for decisions and their consequences
  • Promotes public trust and prevents corruption
  • Example: A company publishing detailed reports about its environmental impact

Interrelationship: These three values are interconnected and mutually reinforcing. Integrity provides the foundation for honesty, and both are enhanced through transparency. Together, they form the cornerstone of ethical behavior in personal and professional contexts.

Piaget's moral development theory
5 Marks

Overview: Jean Piaget's theory of moral development describes how children's moral reasoning evolves as they grow cognitively. He identified two main stages of moral development.

Stage 1: Heteronomous Morality (Approximately 4-7 years)

  • Also called the "morality of constraint"
  • Children view rules as fixed, absolute, and handed down by authorities
  • Moral judgments are based on consequences rather than intentions
  • Rules are seen as unchangeable and must be strictly obeyed
  • Imminent justice is believed - wrongdoings will be naturally punished
  • Egocentric perspective dominates moral reasoning

Stage 2: Autonomous Morality (Approximately 10+ years)

  • Also called the "morality of cooperation"
  • Children understand that rules are created by people
